About Me

Hello! My name is Dan, web developer and student of code. As a former strength and conditioning coach with a Master's in Sport Science, I recently developed a curiosity for technology and code prior to the Covid-19 pandemic. Technology has become essential in daily life and my limited understanding of its inner workings drove me to learn more about it. Diving in without previous knowledge started out as overwhelming but by taking it one step at a time, staying consistent, and help from some fellow coders I have been able to build what I believe to be a solid foundation. I was determined to learn and along the way my curiosity turned to genuine enjoyment. I have chosen to forgo coaching and fully embrace learning to code. The process has been a humbling experience but one that I am so excited to continue with. At this point, my only regret has been not learning to code earlier!

Things I do to improve

Building productive habits allows me to stay focused and maximize my learning. The habits that I have formed have been essential during my coding process and I heavily rely on them to help me grow as a coder and person. A few of my habits:
  • Get code involved everyday. Write, read, learn, talk, or play with code everyday. This keeps me thinking about it all of the time and helps me engrain things I have learned.
  • Read books daily
  • Exercise regularly
Habits are the compound interest of self-improvement. -James Clear
One of the first lessons I learned as a coach was that I don't know everything even if I think I do. I wasn't humble and as a result it closed off my ability to learn. I felt like I knew all I needed to and chose not to learn. I was quickly humbled and have since worked to change my mindset. Be curious, listen, and don't speak in absolutes. There is always much to be learned and many points of views to be considered. Learning has now become a focus of mine and one I embrace daily!

The environment I create for myself allows me to make my habits easier to keep and avoid distractions. Simple tricks such as leaving my code editor open, a book on my nightstand, articles I am interested in reading open on my phone, or even my tv remote in in a closed drawer all contribute to a more productive environment. It allows me to associate my space with a good work flow and limit possible distractions.

This is a rule I found from a book called Grit by Angela Duckworth. I love the idea and have chosen to implement the rule. The rule allows me to find consistency and continue learning and growing, both professionally and personally. The rule has three parts:
  1. Pick an activity/skill/topic of my choice and actively learn and work on it each day. In my case, this is coding but I will find others to incorporate.
  2. No quitting outside of natural cycles or end dates. If I want to quit, not an issue but it needs to be at a natural endpoint.
  3. This is an activity of my choosing.
I believe that becoming an expert requires feedback from others. Learning to embrace feedback isn't always easy but is something I am working on. I choose to believe that those who provide me with feedback have my best interest at heart and that I need to listen and apply it productively. It doesn't always feel good but as long as I can stay present and avoid being defensive then I can listen, integrate that feedback and reflect it back with accountability.
Appreciate what I have and be thankful for the opportunities I have to learn to code. This keeps me humble and keeps me moving forward. The opportunities I have been given and the people who have helped me along the way are rare, reminding me to maximize my chances and hopefully give back to others the same way my mentors have helped me.
